Markets with Asymmetric Information

Consider a simple model with some consumers and some sellers trade for a good. Each consumer can at most purchase 1 unit. Then, we denote the consumer's preference as follows:
$$U=\begin{cases}\theta s-p, \text{ if buys a good with quality } s \text{ at price } p \\ 0, \text{ if no trade} \end{cases}$$

\(\theta>0\) is a 'taste' parameter: how much he cares about the quality. Note also that \(s\in \mathbb{R_+} \). We can then instantly notice that the marginal utility from extra quality is increasing with \(\theta\).

Intuitively, \(s\) is seller's private information, while \(\theta\) is buyer's private information. For now, we focus on \(s\), i.e. buyer cannot observe the quality.

The Lemons Problem

Assume now there are only two agents. Agent 1 (referred as A1) is the seller who owns a car at quality \(s\). Agent 2 (referred as A2) is a potential buyer of the car, and he cannot observe \(s\). Moreover, we use \(\theta_i\) to denote agent \(i\)'s type, where \(i\in\{1,2\}\).

Then we can write down agents' preferences: $$\text{A1:} \begin{cases} \theta_1s, \text{ keep the car} \\ p, \text{ sell the car} \end{cases}$$ $$\text{A2:} \begin{cases} 0, \text{ not buying} \\ \theta_2s-p, \text{ buy the car} \end{cases}$$

A2 only knows the distribution of \(s\). In this example, we assume that \(s\) is distributed uniformly on \([0,\bar{s}]\).

Benchmark (First Best)

For all given \(s\), trade is efficient iff \(\theta_1<\theta_2\), i.e. The buyer's valuation is higher than the seller's valuation. Suppose there is no asymmetric information. Since \(s\) is now observable for both parties, we have trade when: $$ \theta_1s < p \text{ and } \theta_2s-p>0 $$ $$ \Leftrightarrow \theta_1s < p < \theta_2s $$ For all \(s \neq 0\), trade occurs efficiently.

Private Quality (Second Best)

Buyer form expectation on \(s\) given the fact that seller is willing to sell the car: $$ \theta_1s \leq p \Leftrightarrow s \leq \frac{p}{\theta_1}$$

So, by uniform distribution, the buyer's expectation on \(s\) is: $$ \mathbb{E}[s|s\leq \frac{p}{\theta_1}]=\frac{p}{2\theta_1} $$

Then, we can make a comparison and find out: $$p<\theta_1\bar{s} \Leftrightarrow \frac{p}{2\theta_1}<\frac{\bar{s}}{2}$$ which is a example of adverse selection: the high quality car drops out from the market as long as the price is below the level which every seller is willing to sell.

For trade to occur, we need further that the buyer is willing to buy: $$\theta_2\mathbb{E}[s|s\leq \frac{p}{\theta_1}]-p\geq0 \Leftrightarrow \theta_2\geq2\theta_1$$ Now the criteria for trade is stricter in a sense that the taste (type) difference must be great enough to justify trade. In general, there will be less trade than in benchmark case: Too little trade.
